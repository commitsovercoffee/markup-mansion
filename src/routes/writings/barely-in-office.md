## My experience with Remote work

My entire career has been remote, thanks to covid. I was part of the covid batch
in college, we didnt even have placement drive or something. We were all at home,
hoping we would survive. Soon boredom set in so I decide to study to kill time,
found a few remote gigs which I thought I could do for a few months, and after
a few years now I am still doing the same thing. Remote software contracting.

But atleast the beauty in my case is that I get to work on multiple projects and
I also often travel every 3/4 months for workshops, meetups ot tech sprints. Getting
sorta the best of both world.

Below I have shared my thoughts on remote work as this year will mark my 4 years
as a remote software contracting career.

## Nature of Remote Work

First and foremost, one debatable question that comes along when we think about
remote work is whether it works or not, as many people see remote work an outcome
of covid. These same people question productivity of remote workers and wish they
come back to office.

My sense is that software development works well async & hence remotely, open
source software is one proof of this, which has worked even before covid with
distrubuted teams. Even after covid, some of the top companies (like adobe,
facebook, github, slack) in the world have allowed remote work.

I think its safe to say there isn't any drop in productivity in remote work hence it was
allowed. I'm sure these cmopanies didn't give remote work so they could get
rid of their offices and save on rent.

So, are all remote workers productive then ? I don't think so.
Working remotely is a lifestyle choice, it may or may not work for you.

You may need a separate space to focus cause you have 2 toddlers who will crawl up your head
if they saw you still. Or maybe you are a fresh graduate who doesn't mind working remotely
but is missing networking opportunities & hence may not prefer it.

### No More Commute. Yeah !

One key merit of working remotely is that you don't need to commute. Hence, less
exhaustion & you'll save an hour everyday for other things.

Let's say you commute 1.5 hrs in a day, that means 1.5\*5 = 7.5 hrs in a week.
7.5\*4 = 30 hrs in a month which is 4 work days. Or 40 days every year.

Clearly, saving on commute is a relief especially with ever growing traffic and
poorly designed cities.

In India, here are the road metrics :

- 462 people die in everyday.
- 443,366 people get injured everyday.
- India has the highest number of road accident-related deaths in the world.

Your chance of contributing to the number above drops steeply with remote work.

### No More Connection. Nay ?

For most of us, life is school > college > work > retirement > death
Afte school & college, most of our living years are spent in the workplace where
we meet people, some of who become our friend, mentor, besties or even become love of our life.

This is especially true for younger folks who have just gotten out of college and have probably
moved to a new city to work & have no friends. But your workplace can curn out atleast a few friends
or atleast familiar faces.

The point I am trying to make is that, making connection remotely is tough. Most people don't even
like to put on their cameras on meetings and given the schedule no one can spare more time than alotted
to get to know you any better. Hence, chances of non work interaction with a peer are low and hence
bonding or networking with anyone may feel tougher than ever.

I have often heard stories where just showing up has been very beneficial for people. Also, if you
see someone's face everyday I guess you cconnect to the person humanely and which could affect your
view of the person vs someone online.

But sure not everyone wants that. I think the best balance is to work async remotely. But do team meet-ups every 3-6 months.

### Online meetings suck!

Extended hours online talking can be super exhausting, thanks to network glitches, lags & voice drops.
While we can equip ourselves with better tools to reduce technical glitches. Even with all the bells & whistle
of a high end workstation & home office. Staying on hour long calls frankly, just shuts off my brain after a point.
And no, it does not revive even after a ice-breaker game.

But meetings often extend. Why ? Probably because of one or more reasons below :

- Ineffective communication : Not everything needs to be said during the call, some things are
  better said in written form over mail or chat.
- I think only certain complex projects require a audio/video call with screen share. Even those I think can be
  better navigated if people are prepared with docs or writeups or even prior meeting agenda.
  As people may not have context.

Hence, the bad parts of a meeting is probably due to ineffective communication.
Here are some tips to get better at it :

- Keep your calendar up to date.
- Write better mails :
  - Use schedule sends to avoid sending mails at odd hours.
  - Try to share context & be explicit in your speech.
  - Don't trivial queries over mail especially with multiple people CCed.
  - Do not CC people unnesseraily.

Even if you have shakespearce tongue, you may still miscommunicate. Actually,
if you had shakespearce's tongue chances are you _will_ miscommunicate.

Some times you need to be in person to discuss ideas. What also doesnt work super well
is explaining something live on meeting calls, a better way is to do these with recording.

So people can watch it at ease at their own pace. And then maybe have a FAQ call post that.
Or share documentation which is again a key skill people should learn.

But yeah, we can come to consensus back to back extended meetings suck.

### WFH or Remote Work

If you have remote work, and you work from home. You are considered non adventurous
by definition. Why ? Because why are you at home, at ease not chasing wonder lust ?

I think travel is overrated. Sure its fun, but it is backed by capitalism & hence
is glorified to that extent. Travelling is fun, so is meeting new people. But how long
will you do this ?

After few travels, and meetups you will have your intros & ice breakers rehearsde well
enough to impress anyone. And maybe along the way you will pick up some good friends or
the love of your life.

Question is to roam or not to roam. Again personal preferecne and that is the beauty,
that you have the time and opportunity to decide if you wanna stay or go and do either
as long as you like.

### Does Remote work get boring ?

Nope. You do. The more time you have,the more time you have to waste. So its comes to
self disciple to take initiate and follow a schedule to have the best life & not
become complament in the sedentry lifestyle.

Ofcourse you will miss people & so you may need to put some effort to get in touch
with other humans. This is especially true for people working remotely from tier-2 cities
(like mine).

Because most of the youth (including your school/college) friends woould have probably
moved to a tier 1 city for job prospects. So you will have limited crowd.

Sure you can kenjoy the solitude but you;ll also need to balance it, going solo
for too long can cause mania or worse you will become an echo chamber. We need
people around us to give us feedback on our behaviour.

Personally, I have found solace in playing on the field, cycyling to skip my
sedentary lifestyle and I also play online video games from people across the
country whom I plan to meet & go on trips when I want to.

The additional time also gives you space for side quests, be it a side project
or small business or some content creation.

### Remote works better for independent work

This may be one reason why companies often only allow senior position remote
work, since junior may need more guidance day to day which can become a hindrance
in a remote setting.

Especially as fresh graduates, you dont know any better and I;ve heard plenty
stories of how in some companies to get work done, you need to steal time from
other people infact people with hybrid work nature often visit office for the
same purpose, to either expedite their work progress or get mentorship from
someone. I mean good luck, pinging someone over text & waiting for a prompt reply
the same day.

The same thing is possible though in the office, either by walking straight to
the person or talking after office hours. Sooner or later the person may give into
your effort & guide you or mentor you or suggest you a direction.

### Remote work needs self put guard rails

As great as it sounds, we can very easily shoot ourselves in the foot with the
nature of remote work, either by leading a sedentry life which is very bad for
your health, or trying to do everything at once. Even travel & working remotely
without thought can affect your productivity.

Some companies like to measure how much time was spent on the computer, other
have similar stupid metric to measure productivity. I think the best way to
measure productivity is by having reasonably sized teams, and a team lead
who oversees you. Kinda like what is detailed in the book mythical man month.
You need a surgical team.

A smaller team ensures effort visibilty as often something even if effort is put
in, results may not come in. What then ? Do working hours number suffice then ?
nah. Measuring hours or code lines or anything else is plain stupid.

You assign a task, and see if it was met in the mutually agreed deadline
or not and with what quality. And your supervisor can do the qualitative analysis
of your time spent.

Especially in software development, things take longer than expected because
most of the time, the answer is "It depends" for the app your wanna build or
feature you wanna implement. Something will go wrong. Or something will not work,
and you will have to go back to the drawing board.

And hence well put person and profressional guard tails can maximize output
of remote work & its benefits.

# The Nature of Remote Work: Reflections from Four Years of Remote Software Contracting

Remote work has become a hot topic in recent years, particularly as it surged during the COVID-19 pandemic. For many, including myself, this shift marked the beginning of a new career chapter. Having graduated during the pandemic with little hope for traditional placements, I found myself navigating the world of remote work out of necessity. What started as a temporary solution has turned into a fulfilling career as a remote software contractor. Now, as I celebrate four years in this role, I want to share my thoughts on the nature of remote work, its pros and cons, and what it means for professionals today.

## My Experience with Remote Work

My journey into remote work began unexpectedly. As a member of the “COVID batch” in college, I graduated without the usual placement drives. Instead, I was at home, waiting for life to return to normal while battling the boredom of lockdowns. To pass the time, I sought out remote gigs, thinking they would be a temporary solution until the world opened up again. Fast forward a few years, and here I am—thriving in remote software contracting.

One of the biggest advantages of this path is the ability to work on multiple projects and travel periodically for workshops, meetups, and tech sprints. This flexibility has allowed me to experience the best of both worlds: the independence of remote work and the valuable in-person interactions that come from occasional travel.

## The Nature of Remote Work

A significant question surrounding remote work is whether it actually “works.” Many people still associate remote work with the pandemic and question the productivity of remote workers. However, my experience—and that of many others—tells a different story.

**Software Development and Remote Work:**  
Software development, by its nature, lends itself well to asynchronous work and remote collaboration. Open-source projects have thrived with distributed teams long before COVID. Even now, many top companies like Adobe, Facebook, GitHub, and Slack have embraced remote work as a permanent option, indicating that productivity is not only maintained but often enhanced in remote settings.

**Productivity Variability:**  
That said, it’s essential to note that not all remote workers are equally productive. Working remotely is not a one-size-fits-all solution; it is a lifestyle choice that varies from person to person. Some individuals thrive in remote environments, while others may struggle without the structure and social interaction that an office provides.

### No More Commute: The Freedom of Time

One of the most tangible benefits of remote work is the elimination of commuting. Imagine saving 1.5 hours a day—over the course of a week, that translates to 7.5 hours; a month, 30 hours; and annually, that’s equivalent to roughly 40 workdays!

In countries like India, where road safety is a significant concern—462 people die every day due to road accidents—working remotely also means a drastic reduction in the chances of becoming a statistic. The freedom from a daily commute not only saves time but also reduces exhaustion and stress, allowing remote workers to focus more on their tasks and well-being.

### No More Connection?

Despite the benefits of remote work, a notable downside is the challenge of forming connections. In traditional work settings, the workplace often serves as a breeding ground for friendships, mentorships, and professional networks. This is particularly important for younger individuals who have just entered the workforce and may be in new cities without a social circle.

Making connections remotely can be tough, as many employees hesitate to turn on their cameras during meetings or engage outside of scheduled work hours. While working asynchronously can help manage time better, it may also lead to a sense of isolation if team members don’t prioritize bonding activities.

To strike a balance, I believe that teams should consider periodic in-person meet-ups every three to six months. These gatherings can foster camaraderie and strengthen relationships, making remote work more enjoyable and connected.

### Online Meetings: A Double-Edged Sword

While remote work has its perks, online meetings can often feel draining. Extended hours spent talking via video can lead to fatigue, especially when plagued by technical issues like network glitches or audio dropouts.

The effectiveness of meetings can be compromised by several factors:

1. **Ineffective Communication:** Not everything needs to be discussed in a call. Some topics can be better addressed through written communication.
2. **Preparation is Key:** Complex projects may require a meeting, but if participants are not prepared with the necessary context, the meeting may become unproductive.

To improve online meetings, here are some suggestions:

- Keep your calendar updated to avoid scheduling conflicts.
- Craft better emails to provide clear context and minimize unnecessary interactions.
- Use recorded sessions for detailed explanations, allowing team members to review them at their convenience.

### WFH or Remote Work: The Quest for Adventure

There’s often a misconception that working from home equates to a lack of adventure. While the travel and exploration associated with remote work can be exciting, it’s essential to remember that enjoyment is subjective.

Travel is glorified in our capitalist society, but after a certain point, it can become repetitive. The beauty of remote work is the freedom it provides—whether to stay put and focus on work or to roam and explore, the choice is yours.

### Does Remote Work Get Boring?

No, boredom is often a byproduct of poor self-discipline. With ample time at our disposal, it’s easy to fall into a sedentary lifestyle. The key is to establish a routine and engage in activities that challenge and inspire you.

For those of us in smaller cities, where social interaction may be limited, it’s vital to make an effort to connect with others, even if it means stepping out of our comfort zones. Personally, I’ve found joy in outdoor activities like cycling and playing online games, where I can meet people from different regions and eventually plan meet-ups.

Remote work also provides the perfect opportunity to pursue side projects, start small businesses, or engage in content creation—all of which can enrich your life and stave off boredom.

### Remote Work and Independence

Remote work often suits independent roles, which is why companies may reserve it for senior positions. Junior employees, especially fresh graduates, might require more hands-on guidance that is more easily provided in an office setting.

In many cases, junior employees find themselves needing to “steal” time from others in order to get their work done, which can be challenging in a remote environment. Having a hybrid model can help alleviate this, as it allows for easier access to mentors and collaboration.

### The Need for Guardrails in Remote Work

While the benefits of remote work are enticing, it can also lead to unintentional pitfalls, such as leading a sedentary lifestyle or attempting to do too much at once. Setting personal guardrails is essential to ensure productivity and well-being.

Some companies track time spent on tasks, but I believe a better metric for productivity involves assigning tasks with clear deadlines and assessing the quality of the completed work. In software development, where timelines can be unpredictable, this approach is particularly effective.

Ultimately, well-defined personal and professional boundaries can enhance the benefits of remote work, ensuring that individuals can thrive both professionally and personally.

## Conclusion

Remote work has transformed the way we approach our careers, offering both freedom and flexibility. However, it is not without its challenges. By understanding the nuances of remote work, we can navigate its complexities and leverage its advantages to create a fulfilling work-life balance.

As I reflect on my four years in remote software contracting, I’m grateful for the opportunities and experiences it has provided. Whether you're a seasoned remote worker or just beginning your journey, embracing the nature of remote work can open doors to new possibilities.
